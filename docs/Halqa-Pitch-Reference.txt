=== SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM (what to say and how to say it) ===

A transformer explodes in your housing society at midnight. Power goes out.
The street goes dark. You don't know whether to stay inside or leave because
the last WhatsApp message about a nearby robbery was three hours ago and you
cannot tell who in the group still lives on this street. You try to reach the
electricity company. You cannot. You try to check if your neighbours know what
happened. The WhatsApp group is silent because nobody wants to be the person
who cries wolf. By morning the transformer is still sparking, nobody called it
in, and the entire society has lost a night to something that four people with
a working coordination layer could have solved in ten minutes.

This is not one person's story. Three facts validate it at scale:

1. The 2023 nationwide blackout cost Pakistan PKR 100 billion in economic
   losses. That is what happens when communities cannot self-coordinate during
   infrastructure failure at national scale.
2. Only 3% of Pakistanis belong to any formal community organisation (PIDE
   2023). Ninety-seven percent have no structured way to coordinate with their
   neighbours. The gap is not narrow — it is nearly total.
3. In 2026, 46 families in a single Karachi school adopted a privacy-first
   neighbourhood safety system in a single deployment (Pulse Pakistan, 2026).
   This proves the adoption pattern is real when the tool addresses a genuine,
   emotionally salient concern — not convenience, but safety.

Why WhatsApp groups do not solve this: WhatsApp groups already exist in
every Pakistani neighbourhood. They fail for three reasons that are structural,
not fixable. One, WhatsApp cannot verify that participants are actual residents.
Anyone with an invite link joins — former residents, domestic workers, landlords
living elsewhere. Two, WhatsApp cannot structure or preserve information across
time. A report about last week's robbery is unfindable in chat history. Three,
WhatsApp cannot route information to institutions. An alert stays inside the
group. It cannot become a data point that reaches WAPDA or a union council.
These are not feature gaps — they are architectural constraints of a private
messaging system that was never designed for civic coordination.

=== SECTION 2: THE SOLUTION (plain, honest) ===

Halqa is a neighbourhood coordination platform that verifies who lives on your
street, structures what your neighbours report, and surfaces the patterns to
the people who can fix them.

What Halqa is NOT:
- "The Nextdoor of Pakistan" — Nextdoor has been trying to figure out how to
  make money for 15 years and still has not. Do not invite that comparison.
- "An AI-powered community platform" — this is too vague to mean anything.
  Every platform claims AI. Say what the AI actually does instead.
- "Revolutionising civic engagement" — this is too large a claim for a
  hackathon prototype. Say what the prototype actually demonstrates instead.

What to say instead: "Halqa is a verified-neighbourhood coordination tool.
Your street has a WhatsApp group. This is the civic layer alongside it —
for the things WhatsApp structurally cannot handle."

The three-tier verification model in plain language:

Tier 1 — Anyone can join and browse. You pick your neighbourhood, give us your
name and email, and you can see what is being posted. You do not need to prove
where you live. You cannot post yet. This means there is zero barrier to seeing
value.

Tier 2 — You upload a document that shows your address — a utility bill, a
rental agreement, a society card. Our system tries to read the address from the
document automatically. If the confidence is high enough, you are approved
immediately. If not, a human checks it. Once approved, you can post, report
emergencies, and see contact details for service workers.

Tier 3 — This is the trust tier. To reach it, you need two neighbours who are
already verified to vouch for you — the community anchor plus one other verified
neighbour. This unlocks the worker review system. Tier 3 says: this person is
not just a verified address on a document — the community knows them.

=== SECTION 3: THE AI INTEGRATION (what was actually built) ===

We built exactly two AI features. Both are real and working. We do not claim
anything else.

1. Post classification

Every post created in the neighbourhood feed is sent to the Google Gemini
API (Gemma 4 31B) in a background task. The prompt is
Pakistani-specific — it understands terms like "bijli gai", "LESCO", "WAPDA",
"chori", "mohalla", "sadak", "transformer blast", and handles English, Urdu
script, and Roman Urdu interchangeably. It classifies the post into one of
three categories — emergency, community, or general — and assigns a confidence
score (0.0 to 1.0).

If the confidence is 0.70 or above, the system trusts the AI classification
and overrides the user-declared category if needed. Below 0.70, the user's
original category is preserved. This means the AI is an advisor, not a
decision-maker for low-confidence cases.

If the Gemini API is unavailable (timeout, network failure, missing API key),
the post still works — it uses the user-declared category and emergency flag.
The classification is optional infrastructure, not a hard dependency.

In practice, confidence scores for clear emergencies ("transformer blast ho
gaya, bijli nahi hai, please help") reliably come back above 0.90. Ambiguous
posts ("someone is acting strange near the gate") land in the 0.50-0.70 range.
The system handles both.

2. OCR-based address verification

When a user uploads a document for Tier 2 verification, the system sends the
document image or PDF to the same Gemini API with a vision-enabled prompt that
asks it to extract the person's name and address. It also asks: does this
address match what the user told us? The answer is a confidence score.

Three thresholds govern the decision:
- 0.75 or above — auto-approve. The user is verified immediately.
- 0.40 to 0.749 — flagged for manual review by a human (this UI is not yet
  built in the prototype — the record stays pending with reviewer notes).
- Below 0.40 or if the document is unreadable — auto-reject with a specific
  reason (address mismatch, document unreadable, name not found, or invalid
  document type).

The privacy commitment: the uploaded document is deleted from storage after
the verification decision is recorded. We do not keep a copy of your bill or
rental agreement. This is enforced at the service layer — not a preference,
a requirement.

Honest limitation: the manual review queue (0.40-0.749 range) has no UI or
API endpoint in the current prototype. Those records stay pending until a
human checks them via the Supabase dashboard directly. We know this is a gap.
The two automated paths (auto-approve and auto-reject) handle the majority of
cases.

3. Civic signal aggregation (not an AI feature — but the output is smarter
   because of AI)

The civic dashboard takes the individual posts (some classified by AI, some
not) and aggregates them into a neighbourhood intelligence report. It shows:
total reports, emergency count, resolved count, and a breakdown by category
(power, security, infrastructure, water, general) — plus the number of active
neighbourhood members who reported.

The export format is a plain text block that one click copies to the clipboard.
It looks like this (actual format from the codebase):

Halqa Neighborhood Intelligence Report
Green Valley Housing Society, Lahore
Period: [date range]

Summary:
- 47 community reports submitted
- 18 emergency alerts raised
- 11 issues resolved

Reports by type:
- Power/Electricity: 6
- Security: 4
- Infrastructure: 3
- Water: 1
- General: 1

Generated by Halqa - halqa.app
This report represents aggregated, anonymized community data.
Individual reporter identities are not disclosed.

This text can be pasted into an email to WAPDA, shared on a union council
WhatsApp group, or printed. That is what "routing civic data to institutions"
looks like at prototype stage — a human copy-pastes structured text instead
of the institution having to dig through 47 individual WhatsApp messages. The
automated API integration is post-prototype.

=== SECTION 4: WHAT IS ACTUALLY BUILT AND WORKING ===

BUILT AND WORKING (from verified audit results):

- Authentication (email + password via Supabase Auth) — real, working login
  and registration with JWT session management
- Onboarding flow — 5 screens: welcome, neighbourhood search, select
  neighbourhood, register, join. Works end-to-end.
- Tier 1 membership (browse feed, cannot post) — enforced on backend
- Tier 2 verification — document upload, OCR background task (auto-approve
  0.75+, auto-reject below 0.40), pending/approved/rejected states visible
  in frontend with deep-link from notification
- Neighbourhood feed — real-time via Supabase Realtime, 5 seed posts,
  emergency sorting (emergencies at top), category filtering, resolved state
- Post creation — category selector, emergency toggle, body text with
  character count, auto-classified by AI on submit
- Emergency alerts — 2 seed emergencies in the feed, alert banner, AI
  classification badge on each post
- Civic dashboard — metric cards, category breakdown bars, resolution rate,
  recent emergencies list, one-click copy of export text
- Anchor role — status endpoint returns is_anchor, anchor tab shows in
  bottom nav, moderation queue with pending reports, audit log, ability to
  resolve alerts and dismiss reports, two-signature vouching system
- Post reporting — overflow menu on posts lets verified members report
  content, reports appear in anchor queue
- Dashboard snapshot invalidation — creating a new post triggers snapshot
  recomputation (next dashboard load is fresh)
- Error envelope — all endpoints return standardised {"data":...,"error":...}
  response shape
- CORS — works with all required HTTP methods including OPTIONS

Rough edges (working but imperfect):
- All seed posts show "by Unknown" as author (the seed user's profile UUID
  differs from its membership record — cosmetic, not functional)
- Dashboard snapshots are pre-seeded with numbers (47 total, 18 emergencies)
  that do not match the actual 5 seed posts — they correct when a new post
  is created
- The verification rejection deep-link URL lacks a reason query parameter
  (the frontend defaults to "address_mismatch" regardless of actual reason)
- Post list pagination always returns has_more=False (no cursor-based
  pagination implemented)
- AI classification requires a valid GEMINI_API_KEY — if missing, posts
  fall back to user-declared values (graceful but not AI-classified)

DESIGNED BUT NOT BUILT:

- Worker directory (DB schema exists, router commented out in backend)
- Full Urdu interface (Urdu text exists as cultural signal in the UI, but
  there is no complete Urdu/RTL interface)
- Offline/low-connectivity support (PWA service worker registered but no
  offline caching strategy implemented)
- PDF export from dashboard (button exists, shows "coming soon" toast)
- Manual review queue UI for OCR confidence 0.40-0.749 (records stay pending
  until human actions them via Supabase dashboard)
- Push notifications (simulated via Supabase Realtime in-app — no FCM/APNS
  or Web Push implementation)
- Mobile native app (it is a PWA/web app)

If a judge asks "can I see X" and X is not built, say:

1. "Can I see the worker directory?" — "We designed the database and the API
   contracts, but the listing and review system is our post-hackathon priority.
   What you can see today is the foundation it will build on — the dashboard,
   feed, and verification system that the worker directory will depend on."

2. "Can you show me what happens when someone's verification needs manual
   review?" — "The system creates a pending record with the OCR results and
   reviewer notes. In the prototype, an anchor or platform admin reviews it
   through the Supabase dashboard directly — the in-app manual review
   interface is not built yet."

3. "Does the app work offline?" — "The service worker is registered, which
   enables the PWA installation prompt, but we have not implemented the
   offline caching strategy yet. The app requires connectivity in its current
   state."

=== SECTION 5: THE CIVIC ANGLE (why this is civic innovation, not just an app) ===

What to say in 60 seconds (out loud):

"There is a difference between a social app and civic infrastructure. A social
app connects people for conversation. Civic infrastructure gives neighbours a
way to act together — to coordinate during a power outage, to document a
security problem, to hold an institution accountable with structured evidence
rather than scattered WhatsApp messages. Halqa is the second kind of thing.

Most neighbourhood apps fail because they assume trust exists. We built a
verification model that earns trust incrementally — you can see the feed
without giving your address, post after proving your address, and become a
trusted voice only after neighbours who already know you vouch for you. That
is not a feature list. That is a governance model.

And the anchor model — the community anchor — is the most important part. One
person per neighbourhood, elected by the platform (not self-appointed), with a
six-month term, an audit log of every moderation action, and a community
override if twenty percent of verified members disagree with them. This is
not feel-good community management. It is a lightweight but real governance
structure. In Pakistan, where trust in institutions is low and trust in known
neighbours is high, this model works because it mirrors how actual Pakistani
communities operate — through respected local figures, not through anonymous
moderation."

The go-to-market is a civic strategy, not a growth hack:

Phase 1 — Housing societies (DHA, Bahria Town, Gulberg). These are the proof
of concept because address formats are standardised, tech comfort is high, and
the anchor model maps naturally to existing society management structures.

Phase 2 — The mohalla. This is the real impact story. The platform was designed
from the start with the mohalla in mind — document bundles instead of single
standardised formats, Roman Urdu support, the civic dashboard that gives a
voice to communities with the weakest institutional presence.

Phase 3 — The union council bridge. The dashboard export text exists today.
The automated API integration comes next. The goal is that a union council
secretary opens Halqa, selects their jurisdiction, and sees what every
neighbourhood in their area has been reporting — without making 47 phone calls.

=== SECTION 6: COMPETITIVE DIFFERENTIATION (what to say if asked) ===

1. "How is this different from Nextdoor?"

"Nextdoor connects neighbours for conversation. It is a social network
disguised as a neighbourhood app. Halqa is the opposite — it is a civic
coordination tool whose social features exist only to drive adoption. The
verification model, the three tiers, the anchor governance with six-month
terms and logged actions — Nextdoor does not have any of that. It also has
a fundamental trust problem: it never verifies that the person claiming to
live next door actually lives there. Our entire architecture starts from
that problem."

2. "What about WhatsApp — why won't people just use that?"

"They already do. Every neighbourhood in Pakistan has a WhatsApp group. Halqa
does not replace it — it adds a structured, verified layer alongside it for
the three things WhatsApp structurally cannot do: verify that members are
actual residents, preserve information as structured data instead of chat
scroll, and route that data to institutions. WhatsApp is for conversation.
Halqa is for coordination. You will still use WhatsApp for the dawat invite.
Use Halqa when the transformer explodes at midnight."

3. "Google tried this in India with Neighbourly and shut it down — why will
   you succeed where Google failed?"

"Google Neighbourly failed for two reasons. One, it had no verification
model — anyone could answer any question about any neighbourhood, which
eroded trust. Two, it was a Q&A app — ask a question, get an answer — with
no civic output. There was nothing to do on Neighbourly except answer
questions that random people asked. Halqa has a different loop: you verify,
you post, your data gets aggregated into something useful, and that
aggregation gives you a reason to keep participating. The civic dashboard
is what Neighbourly never had — an output that makes individual
contributions add up to something more valuable than the sum of their
parts. We are not claiming we will succeed where Google failed — Google's
failure is a data point we studied, not a guarantee we have solved it."

=== SECTION 8: ANTICIPATED JUDGE QUESTIONS AND HONEST ANSWERS ===

1. "Have you validated this with actual residents?"
   "No formal user interviews. We are a two-week hackathon project, not a
   venture-backed startup with a user research budget. But we have three
   sources of secondary validation: the 2026 Pulse Pakistan deployment (46
   families, same use case, same country), the PIDE data showing the 97%
   gap in civic organisation membership, and the fact that every person on
   this team has lived in a Pakistani neighbourhood and experienced the
   problem firsthand. Post-hackathon, our first priority is a structured
   user research round with one housing society to validate — or disprove —
   our assumptions before building further."

2. "How do you solve the cold start problem?"
   "The community anchor model. You do not need a hundred users for the
   platform to be useful — you need one anchor per neighbourhood who
   already knows the residents. In a housing society, that is the block
   secretary or the most active WhatsApp group admin. They onboard the
   first ten neighbours they trust. Those ten each onboard five more.
   The neighbourhood hits critical mass at roughly forty verified homes —
   the threshold where an emergency alert has a meaningful chance of
   reaching someone who can respond. That is achievable within one week
   of anchor onboarding. The network effect is local by design — we do
   not need every neighbourhood in a city to adopt. We need one
   neighbourhood to adopt fully."

3. "What's your monetization model?"
   "Freemium — the neighbourhood feed and dashboard are free. Revenue
   comes from two things: promoted service worker listings in the
   directory (clearly labelled, never in the feed), and a verified worker
   badge that workers earn through confirmed jobs and ratings — never
   purchased. Honest answer: at prototype stage, our priority is adoption
   and proving the civic value, not revenue. The monetization model
   exists on paper but has not been tested."

4. "How do you protect user privacy given Pakistan's surveillance context?"
   "Four commitments. One: user addresses are never returned by the API
   to the frontend. Only the neighbourhood ID is public. Two: the platform
   does not build behavioural profiles — no tracking of what you read,
   how long you spend, or who you interact with. Three: if a lawful court
   order demands data, we comply, notify the user when legally permitted,
   and log every such request transparently. Four: verification documents
   are stored in a private bucket and deleted after the verification
   decision. No copies kept."

5. "What makes you think people will trust this platform with their home
   address?"
   "They do not have to. Tier 1 — anyone can join and see the feed with
   nothing but a name and email. They give their address only when they
   want to post or access the worker directory. And even then, the address
   is verified by the platform — it is never displayed to other members.
   Your neighbours see your name and that you are a verified resident.
   They never see your flat number or your street address. Trust is built
   incrementally, not demanded upfront."

6. "How does this scale beyond one neighbourhood?"
   "The anchor model replicates. One anchor per neighbourhood, and the
   anchor is the distribution channel — they onboard people they already
   know. We do not need a city-wide marketing campaign. We need to find
   the right person in one housing society, then the next society, then
   the next street. The platform is designed for hyperlocal, street-by-street
   scaling, not viral growth. The network effect is not cross-neighbourhood
   — it is within-neighbourhood density. Forty verified homes in one street
   is more valuable than four thousand scattered users across a city."

7. "Is the AI actually doing anything meaningful here?"
   "Two specific things, both real, both with fallbacks if they fail. One:
   it classifies every post and detects emergencies — this is the thing
   judges can see working live during the demo. Two: it reads addresses
   from verification documents to auto-approve users without manual review.
   But the genuinely novel part is not the AI. It is what happens when you
   combine individual reports — AI-classified or not — into structured
   neighbourhood intelligence. The dashboard export text is the output that
   no existing Pakistani platform produces. The AI is an accelerator, not
   the product."
